# PACING & DIFFICULTY OVERHAUL — FUN CRITIQUE

Reviewer stance: I have shipped city builders and watched players quit them. Everything below is judged on one axis: does it make the next 15 minutes of play more interesting, or does it make the player read a spreadsheet, wait, or guess?

## OVERALL

The diagnosis is right and the centerpiece is right. The current game is a solved toy — free zoning, §424k of grant confetti, and an invisible dice faucet — and the designer correctly identifies that the fix is *visible, ownable* scarcity. Two of the eight mechanics are genuinely excellent: **the migration pool (M3-res)** turns growth rate into a score the player engineers, and **visible construction (M4 stages)** turns the skyline into a story. **Annexation (M1)** is a proven loop (Cities: Skylines tiles, Anno islands) and will land. The materials dilemma (dirty industry vs. import bleed) is the best *decision* in the document.

But the design commits the classic over-corrector's sin: it adds **five new ways to say no and removes almost every way the game says yes**, then papers over the conjunction problem with an aspiration ("one gate should usually bind") that has no mechanism behind it. Count the gates on a single high-density tower start: demand > 4, occupancy ≥ 55%, land-value gate, grid headroom, traffic < 0.72, res pool ≥ ~45, ~72 materials or treasury, site cap, credit freeze, and *then* a dice roll that M8 has deliberately made twice as stingy. That is up to **ten conjunctive "no"s plus RNG on one action**. Hover chips explain one tile's one blocker; they cannot explain a city where the binding constraint rotates every month.

The deeper structural error: **M8 halves the dice odds at the same time the design adds hard gates.** The stated logic — "the RNG still needs slowing so the gates, not the dice, dominate" — is backwards. If the gates are the throttle, the dice must get *more* generous, so that a player who has satisfied every visible condition sees prompt, legible action. As tuned, the early game's binding constraint is the *invisible dice*, not the visible pool (math in TUNING CONCERNS), which recreates the exact "arbitrary RNG faucet" problem this overhaul exists to kill — except now the player also pays fees and watches a pool leak 6% a month while the dice stall.

The shape of the fix is to collapse the gate stack to **four legible axes — people (res pool), money (one unified wallet: fees, land, materials-as-cash, bonds), land (annexation/fullness), and traffic (congestion)** — and cut everything that duplicates one of those axes (biz pool duplicates demand; site cap duplicates materials; halved dice duplicate the pool). Do that, and this is a strong design. Ship it as written, and the mid-game will feel like arguing with a loan officer through a mail slot.

Emotional-budget note: the design cuts §363k of celebration and replaces it with "Wall Street will now underwrite §35,000 of bonds." That is a spreadsheet's idea of a reward. The current game's generosity is partly *emotional*, and the overhaul must replace it with ceremony (completions, first-tower fanfare, parcel-conquest flourish), not just tighter numbers — or "small town growing slowly" will read as "small town being told no."

## VERDICTS

| Mechanic | Verdict | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| M1 Annexation / parcels | **KEEP (simplified)** | Territory purchase is proven fun: directional strategy, recession sales, "the map is visibly not yours." But 289 parcels ≈ 280 near-identical purchases — the decision decays into lawn-mowing by hour two. Go to 8×8 parcels (~72, ~60 buys) or escalating multi-parcel tracts. One decision type should not repeat 280 times. |
| M2 Zoning fees | **KEEP (simplified)** | Cheapest, most legible cost in the doc; the drag-chip running total is exactly right. But drop the `lv·§45` term — land value is already priced into M1's parcel formula (`lv·§120` per tile), so prime land is charged twice for the same thing. Flat per-kind fee, ×2.2 for high density, done. |
| M3 Residential pool | **KEEP — the centerpiece** | "People are the resource" (Banished's lesson) converts wait into engineering. The waiting-families counter as the early-game heartbeat is the best sentence in the design. Protect it: it only works if the dice never mask it (see M8) and the attract score is inspectable (see GAPS). |
| M3 Business pool | **KILL** | Duplicate machinery. Commercial viability is already signalled by demand tracks AND jobsFactor; "one firm per ~6 jobs" is bookkeeping no player will ever feel. Let businesses follow residents via the existing demand system. This single cut removes one full gate from every com/ind/office start and two counters from the UI. |
| M3 Impatience decay (×0.94/mo) | **KILL (or make visible)** | An invisible leak that punishes the player for slow dice and slow crews — things the design itself throttles. Punishing "hoarding" is pointless when hoarding is caused by the game's own gates. If kept, it must be a visible event ("3 families gave up waiting — no homes ready"), which is feedback worth having; as a silent multiplier it is pure hidden cruelty. |
| M4 Construction time + scaffold stages | **KEEP** | Pure fun. The town visibly *gets built*; turbo becomes "watch the boom"; 18s–72s is the right range. This is the mechanic that delivers the owner's "small town growing slowly" fantasy for free. Ship the stage pop-ins, they are load-bearing spectacle. |
| M4 Concurrent-site cap (crews) | **KILL (or make purchasable)** | The one throttle with no dial. You cannot hire crews, prioritize sites, or spend anything to raise it — the "fix" (grow population) is the output it throttles: circular. It is also redundant: materials/import cost already meters simultaneous construction economically. If crews stay, sell them ("hire a construction firm, §X/month") — then it's a decision, not a queue. |
| M4 Stalled site → rubble | **KEEP** | Losses need teeth and this one warns before it bites — but only if the countdown is visible on the chip ("Site failing: 2 months to collapse"). Silent, it will read as a bug. |
| M5 Materials | **KEEP (simplified)** | The industry-vs-imports dilemma is the best standing decision here. But be honest about what auto-import makes it: construction has a cash cost, and local industry is a *discount*. Frame and tune it exactly as that. Cut the global stockpile presentation to one budget line (production offset vs. import bill); a hoarded stock number invites inventory-management busywork the mechanic doesn't need. Hard-block only when the treasury is empty — which is already the money gate anyway. |
| M6 Milestone cut + bond ceilings | **KEEP** | Correct and brave. §424k of confetti was the single biggest reason the current game cannot be lost. Replacing cash with *borrowing capacity* is elegant reward-substitution. But see GAPS: the celebration deleted with the cash must be replaced with ceremony, and the mid-game money curve needs verification (TUNING). Drop the 2% origination fee — noise; rating-tiered interest already prices debt. |
| M6 Bonds + A/B/C rating | **KEEP** | SimCity players expect bonds; the three-tier rating is legible risk with a real bite (C = no new issues). This is the classic "interesting risk" and the §5k blocks keep it chunky rather than fiddly. |
| M6 Credit freeze at 4 red months | **KEEP** | A visible cliff before the death cliff is exactly right — failure you watch approach is drama. Requires loud escalation (ticker at 2 red months, alarm at 3); the freeze must never be the first the player hears of trouble. Watch the death-spiral coupling with M7 (TUNING). |
| M7 Business cycle | **KEEP (simplified)** | Seasons for the long game, recession land-sales, boom-timed bonds — good rhythm. But a ~45-minute cycle means a recession is ~10–15 real minutes of the game saying no for reasons outside the player's control, and its only counter-play is *doing less* (buy land, don't borrow). Needs: a grace period (no recession below ~1k pop / first session), shallower first recession, and at least one *active* recession verb (see GAPS: migration incentives). And decouple its triple-whammy: it currently chokes migration AND surcharges bonds AND (via income) pushes toward the freeze simultaneously — that's the stacked-gate problem in time. |
| M8 Dice nerf (d/140→d/240, d/165→d/300) | **KILL — invert it** | The design's one genuinely wrong idea. With pools, materials, money, and congestion all gating, the dice must get *more generous*, not stingier, so satisfied gates convert to visible action fast. As tuned, early-game starts run ~1 per 35–40 seconds while migrants pile up (math below) — the pool the player is supposed to watch isn't the binding constraint; an invisible coin-flip is. Keep d/140–d/165 or loosen further; consider guaranteeing the first N founding starts bypass the roll entirely so minutes 1–3 always show construction. |
| M8 Fullness retune (dev-weight 0.12→0.5, owned-only) | **KEEP** | Quietly one of the best changes: demand decelerates smoothly as owned land fills, and annexation visibly re-opens the tap. This is what makes M1 feel like buying *demand headroom*, not just tiles. |
| M8 jobsFactor floor 0.45→0.30 | **KEEP** | Mild, directionally right, punishes pure bedroom sprawl without spiraling. |
| M8 Congestion gate on towers (traf < 0.72) | **KEEP** | Exactly what C:S players expect: traffic as a spatial puzzle whose fix is planning, not waiting. Binary threshold is fine *because* the chip names it. Best of the M8 changes. |

## CUTS

Ranked by how much friction each cut removes per unit of fun lost:

1. **The M8 dice nerf.** Restore (or loosen) current odds. Every other mechanic gets more legible the moment the dice stop being a co-throttle. Zero fun is lost — the dice were never fun; they were the thing this overhaul was hired to replace.
2. **The business pool.** Three overlapping signals (demand bars, jobsFactor, firm pool) for one question — "will a shop open?" — is two too many. Kill it and com/ind/office keep their existing, already-tuned gating.
3. **The concurrent-site cap.** Redundant with materials/money, undialable, and it specifically ruins the mass-construction spectacle M4 just paid for. If a crew fantasy is wanted, sell crews; otherwise delete.
4. **Impatience decay as a silent multiplier.** Either surface it as named, countable events or remove it. A hidden 6%/month leak on the player's favorite number is anti-feedback.
5. **The `lv` term in zoning fees.** Land value is priced at annexation; charging it again at zoning is double taxation the player will feel as "the game nickel-and-dimes me" without being able to say why.
6. **The 2% bond origination fee.** Realism-for-realism's-sake; the rating system already does this job with drama instead of arithmetic.
7. **~200 of the 289 parcels.** Not a mechanic cut but a granularity cut: bigger parcels, fewer, weightier purchases.

## GAPS

1. **No aggregate "why is growth slow" view.** Hover chips are per-tile forensics; nobody hovers 400 tiles. The design needs one advisor line / panel: "Last month: 9 starts. Blocked: 6 by migration, 3 by funds, 0 by traffic." This is the single highest-value missing UI element — it is the difference between "I see the bottleneck" and "the game feels arbitrary," and it is the *mechanism* the "one gate should bind" tuning rule needs in order to be checkable at all.
2. **The attract score is a black box.** Six hidden terms decide the game's most important number. Expose the breakdown in the demand panel (jobs +0.2, taxes −0.1, pollution −0.3 …), exactly like C:S info-view tooltips. Without this, "growth rate is a score the player engineers" is false advertising — you can't engineer what you can't read.
3. **No active migration lever — and therefore no recession counter-play.** Tax cuts and parks are slow, spatial levers. Add one direct spend ("regional marketing campaign: +40% inflow for 6 months, §X") — it gives the pool a knob, gives recessions an *active* answer, and is the ordinance-style toggle SC2k veterans expect.
4. **No ceremony to replace the cash.** Completions should sparkle; the first tower, first annexation, and each district build-out deserve fanfare. The design deletes §363k of dopamine and budgets §0 for its replacement.
5. **No progressive disclosure.** Parcels, fees, pools, materials, bonds, and the cycle all hit in minute one. The milestone ladder is *right there*: pools always on; materials narrated at Hamlet; bonds at Village; first recession after the first hour. Five systems introduced one at a time is a tutorial; five at once is a tax form.
6. **No difficulty/sandbox split.** The constants are admitted estimates (Risk 3) headed for a CONFIG table anyway. Ship "Classic/Sandbox" (roughly current rules) beside "Campaign" (this design). It is nearly free and it is the only hedge against the very plausible outcome that v1 tuning is too harsh for half the audience. Players forgive a hard mode; they uninstall a hard game.
7. **Stalled-site and red-month countdowns.** Both cliff mechanics (rubble collapse, credit freeze) need visible fuses — chip countdown and ticker escalation respectively. Specified nowhere in the doc; both are one line of UI and both convert "gotcha" into drama.
8. **8–15 hours is overshooting for a browser toy.** The *systems* deserve 8–15 hours; the *audience* plays 20–60 minute sessions and most will never reach hour three. Target: a satisfying complete-feeling town in 25–30 minutes, a metropolis with all systems singing at 3–5 hours, and full-map fill as an explicit optional capstone beyond that. Keep 8–15h as the 100%-completion tail, not the campaign spine — otherwise bond ratings and business cycles are content most players will never meet.

## TUNING CONCERNS

1. **Early-game start rate contradicts the pitched arc (the big one).** ~20 zoned tiles, 30 samples/tick over 4,624 tiles → ≈0.13 zoned-tile examinations/tick; at M8's p≈0.075 that is ≈0.0097 starts/tick ≈ **one construction start every ~35–40 seconds at 1x** — one house per ~4 game-months. Meanwhile inflow (4–8 families/month) supplies a cottage's `need≈4` every month or two. The pool piles up, decays 6%/month, and the dice — not migration — bind. The "waiting: 7 is your heartbeat" promise fails on the design's own numbers, and Hamlet (150 pop ≈ ~19 cottages) cannot land "minute 4–5": starts alone take ~12 minutes at that rate, before build time and occupancy fill. Fix: restore dice odds and/or bias sampling toward ready tiles; add a guaranteed pioneer wave.
2. **The pool is double-charged.** A start deducts `need = ceil(capGain·0.5)` "committed households," but the completed building seeds only `0.15·cap` and then monthly occupancy fill deducts from the pool *again*. The committed families vanish into neither occupancy nor refund — effective migration cost per capita is roughly doubled, silently. Either seed completed buildings with the committed households or stop deducting at start.
3. **Monthly-refill / per-tick-consumption stutter.** Pools refill in `monthly()` but are consumed by `growthTick` — mid-game the month's pool can be exhausted in the first ticks, giving a start-of-month burst then 8 seconds of nothing, on repeat. Either embrace it (frame as a monthly "construction season," align UI) or trickle the inflow per tick.
4. **Materials prices don't sting when the dilemma matters.** A 72-unit tower imports for §504; the pitched §300–600/month bleed is rounding error against mid-game income. Either imports scale with `S.econ`/city size, or the industry-vs-imports choice is decorative by hour two. Conversely `S.mat += occI·0.7` monthly may trivially swamp demand once industry is large — check both ends.
5. **Recession triple-coupling risks the admitted death spiral.** `S.econ < 0.85` simultaneously cuts migration (M3), surcharges bonds (M6), and depresses income toward red months → freeze — three correlated hits from one hidden sine wave. Decouple at least one (e.g., no bond surcharge below B rating, or migration floor during recessions), and enforce the promised `max(1, …)` inflow floor in code, not in a footnote.
6. **Mid-game money curve is unverified.** Sinks: §400–800k land + zone fees + imports + interest. Sources: ~§60.5k grants + taxes + ≤§250k bond ceiling. Whether hour-two is "leveraged growth machine" or "wait three months to afford the next parcel" is entirely an empirical question — the headless balance harness in Risk 3 is not optional; it is the difference between a difficulty curve and a paywall made of time.
7. **`cityScale = 1 + pop/12000` late-game land inflation.** At 30–40k pop, fringe parcels cost 3.5–4.3× base while the player also faces congestion gates, damped demand, and debt service. Filling "the last quarter of the map" may cross from capstone to chore — consider capping cityScale near 2.5 or exempting low-value fringe parcels so the endgame stays a project, not a mortgage.
8. **Traffic threshold interacts with an untouched traffic model.** `trafMap < 0.72` gates towers, but nothing in this design changes how traffic accrues; if the current model saturates arterials easily, dense cores may hard-lock with no achievable fix, turning the best M8 idea into a wall. Needs playtesting against real `trafMap` distributions at 20k+ pop.
